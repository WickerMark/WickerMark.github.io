EPQ: A COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS OF THE ROLE OF VISUAL AND COMPUTATIONAL METHODS IN THE MORPHOLOGICAL CLASSIFICATION OF GALAXIES USING ASTROPHOTOGRAPHY

EPQ
Machine Learning
Computational Physics
An overview of my EPQ project about classifying galaxies using machine learning.
Author

Mark Watson

Published

August 10, 2024

Introduction

This is an overview of my EPQ project. My EPQ consisted of a comparative analysis, with examples, of the traditional, visual method for classifying galaxies, and a modern computational approach. I captured images of 15 galaxies for the purpose of discussing the pros and cons of the two methods for classification

Criteria for Classifying Galaxies

The three criteria for classifying galaxies are as follows:
  1. The disk-to-bulge ratio of the galaxy
  2. The character of the spiral arms of the galaxy
  3. Stellar populations and H II star-forming regions

Many galaxy classification schemes are built on these principles.

Hubble-De Vaucouleurs Scheme

The Hubble-De Vaucouleurs Scheme for the morphological classification of galaxies is a popular scheme used to classify galaxies. Galaxies are separated into 4 types: spirals (S), lenticulars (S0), irregulars (I), and ellipticals (E).

Spiral Galaxies

The spiral galaxies are further divided into normal or unbarred spirals (SA), intermediate spirals (SAB), and barred spirals (SB) depending on the stage of development of the bar in the centre of each galaxy. The next element of spiral classification is the character of the spiral arms, ‘a’ - ‘d’ is used, with ‘a’ representing tightly bound spiral arms and ‘d’ representing loose arms. The symbols ‘s’, ‘rs’, and ‘r’ are used to denote the development of an external ring structure.

Ellipticals

Ellipticals are denoted by the character ‘E’ followed by an ellipticity between 0 and 7, with E0 being globular nebulae and E7 being the most flattened.

Lenticulars

Lenticular galaxies are said to be “stuck in the middle”, having characteristics of both spiral and elliptical galaxies, yet fitting neither classification.

Visual Methods of Classification

Traditionally, galaxies are classified visually by a team of astrophysicists. By analyzing each individual galaxy using the criteria previously outlined, the astrophysicists give each galaxy an appropriate classification, an extremely time-consuming and laborious. For example, the Third Reference Catalogue of Bright Galaxies was one of the largest attempts to classify galaxies, with galaxies of apparent magnitude 15.5 and below under consideration (11,897 galaxies), conducted by some of the most well-known names in galaxy classification at the time, with the likes of R.J. Buta, H.G. Corwin, and G. and A. de Vaucoueleurs contributing to the classification process.

Justification of a Computational Approach

Large galaxy surveys, such as the APM galaxy survey conducted by Edinburgh-Durham, which reportedly captured images of over 2 million galaxies, and the Sloan Digital Sky Survey emphasise the need for a computational approach to classifying galaxies, as it would be almost impossible for even the most dedicated astrophysicists to classify such a large number of galaxies, while a computer can reliably and quickly output the classification for each galaxy.

Computational Methods of Classification

The Backpropagation Algorithm (ANN)

One example of a machine learning algorithm used for galaxy classification is the ANN Backpropagation algorithm. This algorithm consists of a network defined by 13 entered parameters used as an input layer for the algorithm which is then output as a collection of five galaxy classifications in the output layer.

Any layer connecting the input and output layers is defined as a ‘hidden layer’. Backpropagation algorithms are constructed using nodes “analogous to human neurons”. The network created functions by the nodes in the hidden layer receiving parameters passed from each node in the previous layer and then this information is output to the next layer and the process continues until a classification is found in the output layer.

Locally Weighted Regressions

Another example is Locally Weighted Regressions (LWRs), which can be used to classify galaxies. LWRs belong to the family of instance-based learning methods.

When a galaxy is classified, similar galaxies are identified in training data and this information is applied to the new “query instance”. The LWR finds “an explicit approximation for the target function f over a local region surrounding a query point” which is then used to best classify the galaxy.

Conclusion

This is the conclusion I came to regarding galaxy classificaiton.

Visual methods are most appropriate for classifying galaxies across small data sets and where precision and accuracy are most important, as computational approaches are found to be less accurate.

However, across a large dataset of potentially hundreds of thousands or even millions of galaxies, computational approaches are significantly superior because of the lower time to compute each classification compared to a human expert. Many computational methods are extremely accurate to the nearest family of classification of galaxies, with some Backpropagation algorithms reporting a high success rate and Locally Weighted Regressions reported an accuracy of 91.8%, which is more than suitable for a large survey of galaxies. Also, where a homogenous approach is valued, a computational method is recommended as the algorithm will always output the ‘correct’ classification based on input parameters and so will always be objectively ‘correct’, whereas astrophysicists can disagree on the classification of a galaxy, which is prevalent in the cases of irregular and peculiar galaxies, such as the Cigar Galaxy.

On the other hand, a major advantage of a visual approach is superior accuracy for individual galaxies, as multiple images in different mediums can be analysed to give a more general perspective of the galaxy, whereas a computer can only analyse one image in one medium per galaxy. In the cases of peculiar galaxies that have undergone interactions with other galaxies, often deforming the structure of such a galaxy, N-body simulations are needed to gather more information about the classification of each galaxy, as highlighted in the case of M32, which is not available for a computer program classifying the image, but a human expert is able to take other resources into consideration.

Overall, the best computational approach is using multiple computational methods together to improve accuracy. If an image or area is known to be densely packed with a certain type of galaxy, different computational methods could be used that have an improved accuracy in classifying a certain type of galaxy.
